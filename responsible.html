Responsible Travel

We Leave Places Better

Responsible travel is not a marketing add-on for us — it is the foundation every single programme is built on. Here is exactly how we do it, and what it means for you and for the communities you visit.

Community First

Tourism Income Stays Where It Should

Every guesthouse, lodge, and homestay we use is either community-owned or run by local families. We do not work with international hotel chains or outside operators whose profits leave the country.

On request, we publish a full breakdown of where your programme fee goes — what percentage reaches the communities we visit, what goes to local guides and porters, and what covers our operating costs.

  • →Community-owned accommodation prioritised on every itinerary
  • →Local restaurants and food suppliers only
  • →Local transport operators, not international transfer companies
  • →Craft and souvenir purchases directed to local artisan markets
Our Commitments

Six Pillars of Responsible Tourism

🏡

Community Lodges

We prioritise community-owned accommodation on every route. Tourism income should stay in the village — not in international hotel group accounts.

👨‍🏫

Local Expert Guides

Every guide is a trained local from the same region we visit. No outside operators, no city guides bussed in. The people who know the land best should be the ones showing it to you.

💷

Fair Pay — Always

Every porter, cook, guide, and host is paid above the local living wage. We publish our pay rates on request. Tipping is encouraged but never a substitute for fair pay.

🌱

Low-Impact Routes

We avoid overcrowded trails, use clean transport where available, carry all waste out of remote areas, and brief students on leave-no-trace principles before every trek.

📖

Cultural Briefings

Students receive in-depth cultural and historical briefings before every experience — so their engagement with communities is respectful, informed, and genuinely welcome.

🦁

Wildlife Ethics

No elephant rides. No captive animal encounters of any kind. Wildlife experiences are observation-only, with trained naturalists, from appropriate distances.

Environmental Commitment

Treading Lightly

Nepal's mountain ecosystems and Southern Africa's wildlife habitats are among the world's most fragile — and most threatened. We take our environmental responsibility seriously.

  • ✓Carbon offset contribution included in every programme fee
  • ✓Single-use plastic kit list sent to all travellers before departure
  • ✓All waste carried out from remote trekking and camping zones
  • ✓Low-impact trail selection — avoiding overcrowded routes
  • ✓Eco-briefing included in all itineraries covering local conservation
  • ✓Anti-poaching support partnerships in Botswana and Zimbabwe
What We Don't Do

Lines We Don't Cross

Responsible tourism also means knowing what not to do. These are absolute policies — not guidelines.

🐘

No Elephant Rides

Ever. No exceptions. We visit elephant sanctuaries for ethical observation only.

📸

No Poverty Tourism

We never take travellers to communities as spectators. Every community visit involves genuine reciprocal exchange.

🔒

No Unvetted Orphanages

We do not place volunteers in orphanages without rigorous vetting. Orphanage tourism causes significant harm and we have zero involvement.

🌿

No Single-Use Plastics

Our kit lists are plastic-free. We carry reusable water bottles, filters, and bags on all treks and provide purification tablets.
